What is Generative AI?

AI that creates new content like stories, images, or music by learning patterns from data.

Example: An AI writing a poem or generating a photo of a fictional city.

What are Large Language Models?

AI models trained on huge text datasets to understand and generate human-like text.

Example: A chatbot answering your questions or translating languages.

Section: Ethical Concerns

Exploring how AI impacts fairness, truth, and human values.

4/60

What Are AI Ethics?

Principles to ensure AI is fair, transparent, and respects human rights.

Example: Ensuring an AI doesn’t discriminate in job hiring.

5/60

Bias in Language Models

AI can learn unfair stereotypes from biased training data.

Example: An AI suggesting “engineer” for men and “nurse” for women.

6/60

Example: Bias in Recommendations

A movie streaming AI might suggest action films to men and romances to women based on biased data.

This reinforces stereotypes and limits user choice.

7/60

Misinformation Dangers

AI can create convincing but false information, spreading lies.

Example: An AI writing a fake news story about a celebrity.

8/60

Example: Deepfake Misinformation

AI-generated videos of politicians saying false things can mislead voters.

This erodes trust in media.

9/60

Privacy in AI

AI systems may collect or expose personal data without consent.

Example: A chatbot saving your private messages.

10/60

Transparency Issues

Users often don’t know how AI makes decisions or uses their data.

Example: An AI loan app rejecting you without explaining why.

11/60

Accountability Challenges

It’s unclear who’s responsible when AI causes harm.

Example: If an AI medical tool misdiagnoses, who’s to blame?

12/60

Fairness in AI

AI must treat all users equally, regardless of race, gender, or background.

Example: Ensuring facial recognition works for all skin tones.

13/60

Ethical AI Solutions

  • Use diverse data to reduce bias.
  • Explain AI decisions clearly.

Example: Auditing AI hiring tools for fairness.

14/60

Human Oversight

Humans must monitor AI to catch ethical issues.

Example: Editors reviewing AI-written articles for accuracy.

15/60

Section: Risks in Gen AI

Potential harms from AI misuse or errors.

16/60

Risk: Amplifying Bias

Biased AI can worsen inequality in society.

Example: AI policing tools targeting certain communities unfairly.

17/60

Risk: Spreading Misinformation

AI-generated fake content can spread rapidly online.

Example: A viral AI-written post claiming a false cure for a disease.

18/60

Risk: Overreliance

People may trust AI too much, ignoring its errors.

Example: Students using AI answers without checking facts.

19/60

Risk: Job Displacement

AI automating tasks may reduce jobs in some fields.

Example: AI replacing customer service agents with chatbots.

20/60

Risk: Security Threats

AI can be hacked or used for malicious purposes.

Example: AI creating phishing emails that trick users.

21/60

Risk: Environmental Cost

Training AI models uses massive energy, harming the planet.

Example: AI training emitting as much CO2 as a car’s lifetime.

22/60

Risk: Lack of Rules

Without laws, AI can be misused or cause harm.

Example: Unregulated AI in ads manipulating voters.

23/60

Mitigating Risks

  • Test AI thoroughly before use.
  • Educate users about AI flaws.

Example: Warning labels on AI-generated content.

24/60

Risk: Accessibility Gaps

AI may not work well for all users, like those with disabilities.

Example: Voice AI struggling with certain accents.

25/60

Section: Privacy in Gen AI

Protecting personal data in AI systems.

26/60

Data Collection Issues

AI needs large datasets, often including personal info.

Example: A fitness app sharing your health data with AI.

27/60

Data Storage Risks

Stored data can be hacked or misused.

Example: A chatbot’s database leaking user chats.

28/60

User Consent

Users must agree to how their data is used, but terms are often unclear.

Example: A voice assistant recording you without clear permission.

29/60

Anonymization Challenges

Removing personal details from data is hard and often incomplete.

Example: AI identifying you from “anonymous” survey answers.

30/60

Privacy-Preserving AI

Methods like federated learning keep data on user devices.

Example: A phone’s AI learning your typing habits locally.

31/60

Example: Privacy in Voice Assistants

A smart speaker might record private conversations to train AI.

Users may not know their data is stored.

32/60

Privacy Laws

Laws like GDPR protect user data, but enforcement is tricky.

Example: Fining companies for AI data breaches.

33/60

Building Trust

Clear data policies make users trust AI systems.

Example: An app showing exactly what data it collects.

34/60

Privacy in Healthcare

AI analyzing medical records must protect sensitive data.

Example: A hospital AI leaking patient diagnoses.

Section: Evaluating Gen AI

Checking if AI is accurate, fair, and reliable.

36/60

Evaluation Basics

Measure AI’s performance with tests for accuracy and fairness.

Example: Checking if a chatbot gives correct math answers.

37/60

Testing for Bias

Look for unfair outputs, like favoring one group.

Example: Testing if AI misidentifies people in photos by race.

38/60

Human Evaluation

People review AI outputs for quality and appropriateness.

Example: Rating AI-generated stories for creativity.

39/60

Automated Testing

Software checks AI for errors or inconsistencies.

Example: A tool flagging AI’s factual mistakes.

40/60

Evaluation Challenges

It’s hard to test AI’s “understanding” or real-world performance.

Example: An AI passing a quiz but failing complex questions.

41/60

Benchmark Datasets

Standard tests compare AI models’ abilities.

Example: GLUE tests AI on language tasks like summarizing.

42/60

Real-World Testing

AI must be tested in actual use cases, not just labs.

Example: Trying a chatbot in a busy customer service setting.

43/60

Continuous Monitoring

Track AI performance as it learns or data changes.

Example: Checking if a news AI starts spreading rumors.

44/60

Fairness Metrics

Measure if AI treats all groups equally.

Example: Ensuring an AI loan tool doesn’t reject minorities more.

Section: Limitations of Gen AI

What AI can’t do and why it matters.

46/60

Hallucinations

AI makes up facts or details that aren’t true.

Example: An AI saying “dogs flew planes in 1900.”

47/60

Example: Hallucination in History

An AI might claim Cleopatra used a smartphone, mixing fiction with fact.

This confuses users seeking truth.

48/60

No True Understanding

AI mimics patterns but doesn’t grasp meaning like humans.

Example: AI explaining “love” without feeling it.

49/60

Context Struggles

AI can misinterpret vague or complex questions.

Example: Asking “What’s hot?” and getting weather instead of trends.

50/60

Data Dependency

AI’s quality depends on its training data.

Example: Poor data makes AI give bad recipe suggestions.

51/60

High Costs

Building and running AI needs expensive hardware and energy.

Example: Training an AI costs millions in electricity.

52/60

Ethical Blind Spots

AI lacks moral reasoning or empathy.

Example: AI suggesting a harmful diet without understanding risks.

53/60

Limited Generalization

AI struggles outside its training scope.

Example: A medical AI failing on rare diseases it wasn’t trained on.

54/60

Overcoming Limitations

  • Better data and training methods.
  • Human checks on AI outputs.

Example: Fact-checkers reviewing AI news summaries.

55/60

Limitation: Cultural Nuances

AI may miss cultural or emotional context.

Example: AI translating a joke poorly, offending users.

56/60

Limitation: Creativity Gaps

AI’s creativity is limited to remixing patterns, not true innovation.

Example: AI art copying styles without original ideas.

Section: Future Considerations

How to improve AI for a better, safer future.

58/60

Responsible AI Development

Create AI with ethics, privacy, and fairness in mind.

Example: Global rules to prevent AI misuse in elections.

59/60

Conclusion

Gen AI and LLMs are powerful but need careful handling to avoid bias, privacy issues, and errors.

With ethics and oversight, AI can benefit everyone.

Independent Class Activities: Generative AI and NLP

12 Activities for Communications Students

Critical Thinking, No Instructor Materials (15–20 Min)

Activity 1: Headline Rewrite (Group)

Objective

Rewrite sensationalist headlines to mimic AI-generated content and analyze bias.

Materials

  • Paper and pens
  • Smartphones (optional, to find a news headline)

Steps

  1. In groups of 3–4, pick a recent news topic (or search one online, e.g., “new phone release”).
  2. Write a sensational headline (e.g., “Phone Saves Lives!”).
  3. Rewrite it neutrally (e.g., “New Phone Launched”). Discuss: Is the original biased or misleading?
  4. Share with the class and debate media ethics.

Connection

Relates to Slide 29: Generative AI can produce biased or sensational content, critical in journalism.

Activity 2: Prompt Precision (Solo)

Objective

Refine a vague prompt to improve hypothetical AI output, emphasizing clear communication.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. Start with a vague prompt (e.g., “Write about a city”).
  2. Refine it to be specific (e.g., “Describe Paris’s culture in 100 words”).
  3. Write why the refined prompt would yield better AI output (e.g., more detail).
  4. Share with a partner and discuss prompt clarity in communication.

Connection

Relates to Slide 13: Generative AI requires precise prompts for relevant text outputs.

Activity 3: Deepfake Scenario Design (Group)

Objective

Create and evaluate a fictional deepfake scenario for ethical risks in media.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. In groups of 3–4, create a fictional deepfake scenario (e.g., “A deepfake of a politician endorses a product”).
  2. List 2 potential harms (e.g., misinformation, reputational damage).
  3. Propose 1 solution (e.g., public awareness campaigns).
  4. Share with the class and discuss media ethics.

Connection

Relates to Slide 30: Deepfakes pose ethical challenges in communication, like misinformation.

Activity 4: Ad Slogan Analysis (Solo)

Objective

Analyze a student-created ad slogan for AI-like personalization and bias.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. Write a slogan for a product (e.g., “Run Fast with Our Shoes!”).
  2. Analyze: Is it personalized (e.g., targets runners)? Any biases (e.g., assumes athleticism)?
  3. Revise to be more inclusive (e.g., “Move Your Way with Our Shoes!”).
  4. Share with the class and discuss AI’s role in marketing.

Connection

Relates to Slide 21: AI-generated marketing content needs scrutiny for bias and inclusivity.

Activity 5: Chatbot Role-Play (Group)

Objective

Simulate a chatbot conversation and critique its tone for customer communication.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. In groups of 3–4, one student acts as a customer (e.g., “My order is late”), others as a chatbot.
  2. Write 3 chatbot responses, aiming for robotic tone (e.g., “Order delayed. Wait.”).
  3. Revise responses to be empathetic (e.g., “Sorry for the delay, it’s on its way!”).
  4. Discuss: Why does tone matter in AI communication?

Connection

Relates to Slide 48: NLP-powered chatbots need empathetic tone for effective customer service.

Activity 6: Sentiment Guess (Solo)

Objective

Predict NLP sentiment analysis for a student-written review and discuss potential errors.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. Write a short product review (e.g., “Amazing food, but slow service”).
  2. Predict: Would an AI label it positive, negative, or mixed? Why?
  3. Share with a partner and discuss: Could sarcasm or context confuse the AI?
  4. Discuss: How does this affect customer feedback analysis?

Connection

Relates to Slide 62: NLP struggles with sarcasm and context in sentiment analysis.

Activity 7: Translation Mix-Up (Group)

Objective

Create and fix a mock AI translation error based on idioms, exploring NLP challenges.

Materials

  • Paper and pens
  • Smartphones (optional, to look up idioms)

Steps

  1. In groups of 3–4, pick an English idiom (e.g., “Kick the bucket”).
  2. Write a literal “AI” translation to another language (e.g., “Golpear el cubo” in Spanish).
  3. Fix it to convey the correct meaning (e.g., “Morir” for “die”).
  4. Share and discuss: Why do idioms challenge AI translation?

Connection

Relates to Slide 45: Machine translation struggles with cultural context like idioms.

Activity 8: News Summary Critique (Solo)

Objective

Summarize a news article and compare with a peer’s to mimic AI summarization challenges.

Materials

  • Paper and pens
  • Smartphones to find a short news article online

Steps

  1. Find a short news article online (e.g., on BBC or CNN, ~200 words).
  2. Write a 1-sentence summary of the main point.
  3. Compare with a partner’s summary: Are key points missed? Any biases?
  4. Discuss: How might AI summarization differ?

Connection

Relates to Slide 46: NLP text summarization must capture key points accurately.

Activity 9: Social Media Audit (Group)

Objective

Analyze a public social media post for AI-detectable issues like hate speech.

Materials

  • Smartphones to browse public social media (e.g., X, Instagram)
  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. In groups of 3–4, find a public social media post (e.g., a comment on a news page).
  2. Analyze: Could an AI flag it as harmful (e.g., offensive language)? Why?
  3. Write a brief justification for flagging or not flagging it.
  4. Share and discuss: Can AI moderate social media effectively?

Connection

Relates to Slide 55: NLP detects harmful content in social media but needs human judgment.

Activity 10: Bias Spotter (Solo)

Objective

Write a job description and check for AI-like biases, focusing on inclusive communication.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. Write a short job description (e.g., for a graphic designer, 3 sentences).
  2. Check for biases (e.g., “young, energetic” excludes older candidates).
  3. Revise to be inclusive (e.g., “creative and motivated”).
  4. Share with the class and discuss AI’s bias risks.

Connection

Relates to Slide 31: Generative AI can perpetuate biases in communication outputs.

Activity 11: AI Journalism Vision (Group)

Objective

Forecast AI’s impact on journalism, analyzing benefits and risks.

Materials

  • Paper and pens
  • Smartphones (optional, to research AI in journalism)

Steps

  1. In groups of 3–4, brainstorm: How might AI change journalism in 5 years?
  2. List 2 benefits (e.g., automated fact-checking) and 2 risks (e.g., fake news).
  3. Share predictions with the class.
  4. Discuss: How should journalists prepare for AI?

Connection

Relates to Slide 32: The future of Generative AI includes media applications.

Activity 12: Ethics Dilemma (Solo)

Objective

Propose solutions for a student-created AI ethics scenario in communication.

Materials

  • Paper and pens

Steps

  1. Write a brief AI ethics scenario (e.g., “An AI writes false news about a company”).
  2. Analyze: What’s the harm? Who’s affected?
  3. Propose 1 solution (e.g., mandatory AI content labeling).
  4. Share with the class and discuss ethical communication.

Connection

Relates to Slide 29: Ethical challenges in Generative AI require communication strategies.

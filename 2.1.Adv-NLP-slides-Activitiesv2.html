In-Class Activities: NLP for Communications

12 Activities to Explore How Machines Understand Language

Use your phone or laptop for quick research. Work in groups and share ideas!

Activity 1: Tokenization in Social Media

Task: Pick a short social media post (e.g., from X or Instagram, 1-2 sentences). Break it into individual words (tokens) manually. Discuss how a machine might use these tokens to understand the post’s topic.

Steps:

  • Find a post (e.g., “I love my new phone! #tech”).
  • List the tokens (e.g., “I”, “love”, “my”, “new”, “phone”, “#tech”).
  • Discuss: What can a machine learn from these tokens? (e.g., positive sentiment, tech-related).
  • Think: Could hashtags or emojis cause problems for tokenization?

Example: Post: “Great coffee at Starbucks!” → Tokens: “Great”, “coffee”, “at”, “Starbucks”. A machine might use “coffee” and “Starbucks” to tag this as a café review.

Share: One group member explains your tokens and insights (2 min).

Activity 2: Stop Words in News Headlines

Task: Find a news headline online (e.g., BBC, CNN). Identify stop words (e.g., “the”, “is”) and remove them. Discuss how this affects the headline’s meaning for a machine analyzing it.

Steps:

  • Choose a headline (e.g., “The economy is growing fast”).
  • List stop words (e.g., “The”, “is”) and rewrite without them (e.g., “economy growing fast”).
  • Discuss: Does removing stop words help or hurt understanding? Why?
  • Think: In what cases might stop words be important (e.g., “not” in “Economy is not growing”)?

Example: Headline: “Apple launches new iPhone” → Stop words: “new” → Without stop words: “Apple launches iPhone”. Machine focuses on “Apple” and “iPhone” for topic tagging.

Share: Share your headline and analysis (2 min).

Activity 3: Stemming vs. Lemmatization

Task: Choose 5 words from an advertisement (e.g., on a website or social media). Apply stemming and lemmatization manually (guess the base form). Discuss which method is better for ad analysis.

Steps:

  • Find an ad (e.g., “Running shoes for runners”).
  • List words (e.g., “running”, “runners”). Stem (e.g., “run”, “runner”) and lemmatize (e.g., “run”, “runner”).
  • Discuss: Does stemming (faster) or lemmatization (accurate) help machines understand ad keywords better?
  • Think: Could stemming cause errors (e.g., “studies” → “studi”)?

Example: Ad: “Discover better living” → “better” → Stem: “bett”; Lemmatize: “good”. Lemmatization helps machines link “better” to positive vibes.

Share: Explain your words and choice (2 min).

Activity 4: POS Tagging in Tweets

Task: Find a tweet or short post on X. Label each word’s part of speech (e.g., noun, verb). Discuss how this helps machines analyze social media trends.

Steps:

  • Choose a tweet (e.g., “Love my new car”).
  • Label POS (e.g., Love: verb, my: pronoun, new: adjective, car: noun).
  • Discuss: How does knowing “car” is a noun help machines spot trending products?
  • Think: What challenges arise with slang or emojis?

Example: Tweet: “Amazing concert tonight” → Amazing: adjective, concert: noun, tonight: adverb. Helps machines tag “concert” as an event.

Share: Share your tweet and POS labels (2 min).

Activity 5: Named Entity Recognition in Press Releases

Task: Find a short press release online (e.g., company website). Identify named entities (e.g., people, companies). Discuss how NER helps journalists summarize news.

Steps:

  • Find a press release (e.g., “Tesla opens factory in Shanghai”).
  • List entities (e.g., Tesla: Organization, Shanghai: Location).
  • Discuss: How does NER help journalists quickly find key facts?
  • Think: Could ambiguous names (e.g., “Apple” as fruit vs. company) cause errors?

Example: “Google hires Jane Doe” → Google: Organization, Jane Doe: Person. NER helps tag articles for quick news summaries.

Share: Share your entities and insights (2 min).

Activity 6: TF-IDF in Blog Posts

Task: Find a short blog post (e.g., on Medium). Pick 3 words and guess which are most important using TF-IDF logic (frequent in the post, rare elsewhere). Discuss how this helps content marketing.

Steps:

  • Choose a blog post (e.g., about fitness).
  • Pick words (e.g., “yoga”, “health”, “the”). Guess which have high TF-IDF (e.g., “yoga” is frequent and unique).
  • Discuss: How does TF-IDF help marketers choose keywords for SEO?
  • Think: Could common words like “health” be less useful?

Example: Post about “AI trends” → “AI” and “trends” have high TF-IDF, “the” has low. Helps marketers target “AI” for ads.

Share: Share your words and analysis (2 min).

Activity 7: Word Embeddings in Ads

Task: Find an online ad (e.g., Google Ads). Pick a key word and guess related words a machine might link to it using embeddings. Discuss how this helps target audiences.

Steps:

  • Find an ad (e.g., “Buy luxury cars”).
  • Choose a word (e.g., “luxury”). Guess related words (e.g., “premium”, “exclusive”).
  • Discuss: How do embeddings help ads reach people searching for “premium”?
  • Think: Could unrelated words (e.g., “luxury” → “hotel”) cause bad targeting?

Example: Ad: “Travel in style” → “style” → Related: “fashion”, “elegance”. Helps target fashion-conscious travelers.

Share: Share your word and related words (2 min).

Activity 8: BERT in Reviews

Task: Find a product review (e.g., Amazon, Yelp). Identify a sentence with mixed sentiment (e.g., “Great food, bad service”). Discuss how BERT understands both parts and its use in PR.

Steps:

  • Find a review (e.g., “Phone is fast but battery dies quickly”).
  • Identify mixed sentiment (e.g., “fast” is positive, “dies” is negative).
  • Discuss: How does BERT separate these for a PR team to respond?
  • Think: Could BERT miss sarcasm (e.g., “Great service, NOT!”)?

Example: Review: “Tasty pizza, slow delivery” → BERT sees “tasty” as positive, “slow” as negative. Helps PR focus on delivery issues.

Share: Share your sentence and analysis (2 min).

Activity 9: Word Sense Disambiguation

Task: Find a sentence online with an ambiguous word (e.g., “bank”). Determine its meaning based on context. Discuss how this helps accurate news reporting.

Steps:

  • Find a sentence (e.g., “Fish swam by the bank”).
  • Identify the word’s meaning (e.g., “bank” = riverbank).
  • Discuss: How does disambiguating “bank” ensure clear news stories?
  • Think: What happens if a machine picks the wrong meaning?

Example: “She works at the bank” → “bank” = financial institution. Ensures news about finance, not rivers.

Share: Share your sentence and meaning (2 min).

Activity 10: Coreference in Interviews

Task: Find a short interview snippet (e.g., online article). Identify a pronoun and what it refers to. Discuss how this helps journalists summarize interviews.

Steps:

  • Find a snippet (e.g., “Elon Musk spoke about Tesla. He said it’s innovative”).
  • Identify pronoun (e.g., “He” = Elon Musk, “it” = Tesla).
  • Discuss: How does linking pronouns help summarize who said what?
  • Think: Could ambiguous pronouns (e.g., “he” in a group interview) cause errors?

Example: “Jane loves her job” → “her” = Jane. Helps summarize Jane’s job satisfaction clearly.

Share: Share your pronoun and reference (2 min).

Activity 11: Intent Detection in Chatbots

Task: Write a short customer service query (e.g., for a website chatbot). Guess the intent (e.g., question, complaint). Discuss how this helps customer communication.

Steps:

  • Write a query (e.g., “Where’s my order?”).
  • Guess intent (e.g., Question).
  • Discuss: How does detecting intent help chatbots respond better?
  • Think: Could vague queries (e.g., “Help!”) confuse the chatbot?

Example: Query: “Cancel my subscription” → Intent: Command. Helps chatbot process the cancellation quickly.

Share: Share your query and intent (2 min).

Activity 12: Ethics of NLP in Media

Task: Research a recent NLP use in media (e.g., AI-generated articles, sentiment analysis on X). Discuss potential ethical issues (e.g., bias, misinformation).

Steps:

  • Search for an example (e.g., Google “AI in journalism 2025”).
  • Identify an NLP use (e.g., auto-generated sports reports).
  • Discuss: What ethical concerns arise? (e.g., Could AI misreport facts?)
  • Think: How can communicators ensure ethical NLP use?

Example: AI summarizing X posts for news might amplify biased opinions. Communicators should check sources manually.

Share: Share your example and ethical concerns (2 min).
